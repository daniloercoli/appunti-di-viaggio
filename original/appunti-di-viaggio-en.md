# Smart Working vs. Remote Working vs. Hybrid Working {#smart-working-vs.-remote-working-vs.-hybrid-working}

**Understanding the Differences and Their Implications**

The COVID-19 pandemic has significantly reshaped the global work landscape. Social distancing measures and travel restrictions forced many organizations to quickly revise their operational methods to ensure business continuity. As a result, remote work became the new normal for many companies and employees, accelerating an already emerging trend toward more flexible work arrangements. This situation led to the emergence and widespread adoption of *various types of smart working*, each with its own specific characteristics and implications. Among the different forms that exist, the most common and widely adopted are traditional **smart working**, **remote work**, and **hybrid work**. These models represent distinct approaches to workplace flexibility, and we will explore each of them to gain a clearer understanding of their specific features and how they can be adapted to different work environments.

**Smart working** is an innovative approach that emphasizes flexibility and freedom for employees. This style of work is defined by its focus on results and objectives rather than the time spent in the office. Enabled by technology, smart working allows employees to work anytime and from anywhere, fostering a high degree of trust by granting them greater autonomy and expecting them to take responsibility for their own work.

On the other hand, **remote work** is a form of smart working that involves working from a location outside the office while still maintaining standard working hours. Remote workers may work from home, coworking spaces, or any other place with an internet connection. Remote work can be a temporary or permanent arrangement, depending on organizational needs and policies.

**Hybrid working** combines traditional in-office work with remote work, with employees alternating between the two. They are typically required to be on-site for 2-3 days and work from home for another 2-3 days, while still adhering to standard office hours in both scenarios. This means that both organizations and employees must learn to operate effectively in both environments. They also need to manage and adapt to both face-to-face and virtual communication, which can pose an additional challenge for team coordination and cohesion.

Although hybrid working offers a degree of flexibility by reducing lengthy daily commutes, it does not encompass all the features of smart working. In fact, by merging two distinct worlds, it often fails to offer the best of either. Companies often view the hybrid setup as a perk granted to employees rather than a genuine structural change in their way of working, which can limit the effectiveness and potential benefits of this model.

Both smart working and remote work have advantages and disadvantages that organizations must consider before deciding which approach to adopt. Smart working can promote work-life balance by allowing employees the freedom to work from home or other locations, enabling them to spend more time with family or pursue hobbies. It can also reduce employer costs since it is not necessary to provide physical space for every employee, and it can help companies attract and retain talent from different parts of the world, increasing workforce diversity and broadening perspectives. Additionally, remote work allows employees to work in a comfortable, familiar environment, which can boost productivity.

However, smart working can pose challenges for some employees, requiring a high degree of self-discipline due to potential distractions at home. Remote work can also feel isolating, as employees may miss the social aspects of working in an office, leading to feelings of loneliness and disconnection.

Understanding the differences between smart working, remote work, and hybrid work is crucial for organizations looking to adapt their methods in an ever-evolving landscape. By weighing the benefits and challenges of each approach, organizations can implement the most suitable strategies to optimize their operations and maintain employee satisfaction and productivity.

## Key Areas {#key-areas}

Effective leadership, particularly in the context of managing remote teams, requires a diverse set of skills to navigate the challenges of virtual collaboration. The following key areas provide a roadmap to building and refining the competencies necessary to lead with impact and adaptability in a remote environment. Each area highlights critical practices that foster team cohesion, productivity, and personal growth.

**Emotional Intelligence:** A fundamental aspect of effective leadership, emotional intelligence refers to the ability to understand and manage both your own emotions and those of your team members. This skill becomes even more crucial when leading remote teams, where non-verbal communication is often lacking. To enhance emotional intelligence, one must develop several key sub-skills, such as active listening, empathy, and self-awareness. Active listening means not just hearing but genuinely grasping your team members' thoughts and feelings, which helps build strong rapport and trust. Empathy enables you to connect with your team on a personal level, recognizing their feelings and viewpoints. This is especially vital in a remote environment, where physical distance can lead to feelings of isolation. Finally, self-awareness involves recognizing your own emotions, strengths, and weaknesses. Being aware of your limitations can enhance collaboration with your team and encourage you to seek their expertise when necessary.

**Clear Expectations:** Setting and communicating clear expectations is essential for managing remote teams. These expectations should encompass performance metrics, behavior, and communication standards. For example, you might define specific goals for individual team members, establish guidelines for professional conduct, or set rules regarding how and when team members should interact with one another. Clearly articulating these expectations can help avoid misunderstandings and ensure that everyone is working towards the same objectives.

**Effective Delegation:** Delegation is a vital skill for any leader, but it takes on a different form in remote teams. As the team manager, you are tasked with assigning responsibilities in a way that aligns with each member's strengths and weaknesses. By understanding the unique skills and capabilities of your team, you can delegate tasks that allow each individual to excel and deliver their best work. This not only boosts productivity but also cultivates a sense of achievement.

**Decision-Making:** The role of a manager involves making numerous decisions, often with limited information. In these situations, it's essential to gather as much information as possible and consider different viewpoints. This may include seeking input from your team or consulting with other leaders. It's also important to remember that making mistakes is a natural part of the process. The key is to learn from these mistakes and use them as opportunities for better decision-making in the future.

**Conflict Resolution:** In any team, conflicts are bound to happen. They can stem from differences in personalities, work styles, or opinions. As a manager, you have the responsibility to mediate these conflicts and promote open, respectful communication. Encourage your team to share their thoughts and feelings candidly, and help them work towards finding common ground or a compromise.

**Continuous Learning:** The world of management is ever-changing, which means you need to continuously update your knowledge and skills. Be receptive to feedback, take advantage of growth opportunities, and aim for ongoing improvement. This could involve attending workshops or training sessions, reading books or articles on leadership, or seeking advice from mentors. You might also think about enrolling in online courses or joining professional networking events. These activities not only enhance your skills but also introduce you to new ideas and perspectives.

Managing a remote team requires a unique combination of skills and practices; by spending time on these essential areas, you can establish a solid base for your leadership journey. Keep in mind that the path to becoming an effective team leader is one of ongoing learning and flexibility. Look to resources like the Harvard Business Review, Forbes, or Manager Tools for valuable guidance and inspiration along the way.

## Communication {#communication}

Effective communication is the cornerstone of any successful team, but it becomes absolutely critical in a remote setting. Without the benefit of face-to-face interactions, teams must rely on intentional and well-structured communication strategies to stay connected, aligned, and productive. Regular updates on current projects, organizational changes, and general check-ins are vital to ensure that everyone is on the same page and feels part of the team.

In a remote environment, the absence of casual hallway conversations and impromptu desk visits means that important information can easily slip through the cracks. Misunderstandings may arise due to the lack of non-verbal cues and immediate feedback, leading to confusion and delays. To mitigate these challenges, it's essential to establish clear communication channels and protocols.

### Hybrid calls {#hybrid-calls}

In hybrid meetings, where some attendees are physically present in the office while others join remotely, it is crucial to ensure a fair and engaging experience for everyone. A proper technical and organizational setup can make the difference between an effective meeting and a frustrating one.

**Using Individual Equipment**

To ensure clear communication and active participation, each attendee should use their own camera, headphones, and screen. When multiple people share the same device in a meeting room, several issues arise for remote participants:

* **Poor audio quality**: Remote attendees may struggle to hear speakers clearly because shared microphones might not pick up all voices equally.  
* **Background noise**: A single shared microphone often remains active, capturing ambient noise that can distract and make it difficult to follow the conversation.  
* **Limited visibility**: Facial expressions are fundamental to effective communication. With just one shared camera, the faces of in-person attendees appear small on screen, making it hard to catch nonverbal cues.  
* **Confusion about who’s speaking**: When the screen shows multiple people at once, it becomes harder for remote participants to identify the current speaker, especially without clear visual indicators.  
* **Difficulty joining the conversation**: Audio delays and lack of direct visual signals make it challenging for remote attendees to jump into the conversation at the right moment.

**Limitations for In-Person Attendees**

Those in the room who share a single setup also face challenges due to the lack of individual equipment:

* **Limited screen sharing**: They cannot easily share their own presentations or documents without interrupting the meeting flow.  
* **Viewing difficulties**: The shared screen may be far away or too small, making it difficult to see important details during presentations.  
* **Reduced interaction with content**: Without direct access to shared documents, they cannot scroll or explore information at their own pace.  
* **Restricted participation in collaborative documents**: They cannot actively contribute to shared documents in real time, such as taking notes or adding comments during the meeting.

The disadvantages for remote participants often go unnoticed by those physically present, yet they can be significant. This disparity can lead to feelings of exclusion or frustration, sometimes prompting people to travel just to attend meetings in person.

**Challenges of Sharing Physical Spaces with Individual Equipment**

At first glance, having multiple people in the same room each using their own devices might seem like a solution, but in practice, it introduces further complications:

* **Audio interference**: Hearing someone speak both directly and through a device can create distracting echoes and delays.  
* **Microphone management**: It’s vital to remember to mute microphones when not speaking to avoid picking up ambient noise or other voices.  
* **Additional distractions**: Multiple individuals with active devices in the same space can cause confusion and increase background noise.

**Best Practices for Effective Hybrid Meetings**

To address these challenges and improve the hybrid meeting experience, you can adopt the following strategies:

* **Dedicated spaces**: Encourage in-office participants to join from individual workstations or conference rooms equipped with advanced videoconferencing technology.  
* **Appropriate technology**: Invest in directional microphones, high-resolution cameras, and noise-cancellation systems to enhance audio and video quality.  
* **Training**: Educate employees on best practices for effective hybrid meetings, including managing equipment and communication platforms.  
* **Meeting protocol**: Establish clear guidelines—such as using visual cues to request speaking time, scheduling regular breaks for input, and practicing active moderation—to engage all participants.

### Meetings with clients {#meetings-with-clients}

When preparing for a meeting, create a **shared agenda** in a collaborative document—such as Google Docs or a live Asana board—and link it directly in the meeting description. Even if the agenda simply lists Asana card titles with their links, this ensures that both you and the client can contribute topics and see the day’s discussion points at a glance.  
A key consideration is to **time-box** the meeting, especially during early project modeling sessions. When the allocated time expires, politely note that you are exceeding the scheduled slot and ask if everyone can continue. For longer, previously planned meetings that last more than 90 minutes, schedule a **short five-minute break** to allow participants to stretch, grab water, and return with fresh focus. You can even play light background music and display an intermission screen. Avoid rushing through the final minutes to cover every point—any unfinished items can be revisited during the next session.  
After the call, **document a concise summary** in the shared agenda and update any relevant Asana cards with specific details or action items. This written record ensures clarity on outcomes and next steps for everyone.  
On the technical side, **proper lighting, clear video, and crisp audio** go a long way toward establishing a professional atmosphere and building client trust. Choose a well-lit area with minimal shadows, and invest in quality headphones or a headset for more reliable sound. If you are joining from a shared office, make sure each participant has their own workstation, **headphones, and camera rather than crowding around a single device**. This avoids audio feedback issues, ensures everyone is visible, and makes active participation easier.  
By combining a structured agenda, disciplined time management, clear documentation, and high-quality audio/video setups, you can create smoother, more engaging remote meetings that respect everyone’s time and foster a positive, collaborative relationship with your clients.

### One-on-One Meetings {#one-on-one-meetings}

One-on-one meetings are essential for effective leadership and play a key role in fostering strong relationships between leaders and their team members. These meetings encourage open communication, create opportunities for feedback and collaboration, and enhance team performance, engagement, and job satisfaction. We will explore the significance of one-on-one meetings, along with practical tips, best practices, and strategies for conducting meaningful and impactful discussions.

#### The Importance of One-on-One Meetings

One-on-one meetings serve as a cornerstone of effective leadership, fostering trust and rapport between leaders and their teams. By dedicating focused time to address individual concerns, goals, and challenges, leaders signal their commitment to each team member’s personal and professional growth. These interactions allow both parties to better understand one another’s work styles, personalities, and preferences, paving the way for stronger collaboration and team cohesion.

Beyond building relationships, these meetings enhance communication by creating a safe space for open dialogue. Sensitive topics, misunderstandings, or innovative ideas that might feel daunting to raise in group settings can be discussed candidly. This transparency not only helps preempt potential conflicts but also cultivates a culture of trust, creativity, and collective problem-solving.

Regular check-ins also enable leaders to monitor progress and align expectations. By reviewing milestones, identifying areas for improvement, and setting actionable goals, leaders ensure their team remains focused on high-priority tasks and stays on track to achieve objectives. This iterative process of refining goals keeps everyone accountable and forward-driven.

Equally vital is the role of feedback and recognition in these meetings. Constructive, timely input helps individuals grow, while acknowledging achievements—no matter how small—boosts morale, motivation, and job satisfaction. Celebrating successes reinforces a sense of value and purpose.

Finally, one-on-ones act as a springboard for growth. Leaders can pinpoint opportunities for skill development, whether through training, mentorship, or stretch assignments. By tailoring these discussions to individual aspirations, leaders empower their team members to unlock their potential, advance their careers, and contribute meaningfully to the organization’s mission.

#### Ten Best Practices for One-on-One Meetings

1\. Schedule Regular Meetings: Establish a consistent meeting schedule, such as weekly or biweekly, to maintain open lines of communication and ensure that issues are addressed promptly. Be flexible with the schedule to accommodate team members' workloads and other commitments, but prioritize these meetings as an essential part of your leadership responsibilities.

2\. Prepare an Agenda: Encourage team members to prepare an agenda for each meeting, outlining the topics they would like to discuss. This ensures that meetings are focused and productive and allows leaders to prepare their thoughts and feedback in advance. Additionally, leaders should also consider adding their own items to the agenda to address broader team or organizational issues, share updates, or discuss career development opportunities.

3\. Create a Safe Space: Foster a supportive and confidential environment where team members feel comfortable discussing their concerns, challenges, and aspirations. This includes being empathetic, non-judgmental, and open to receiving feedback about your own leadership style or decisions.

4\. Be Fully Present and Engaged: Give your full attention during the meeting by minimizing distractions, actively listening, and responding thoughtfully to your team member's comments and concerns. This demonstrates your commitment to their success and shows that you value their time and input.

5\. Provide Constructive Feedback: Offer specific, actionable feedback that helps your team members understand their strengths and areas for improvement. Be sure to balance constructive criticism with recognition of their achievements, and be mindful of the timing and delivery of your feedback.

6\. Focus on Solutions and Action Steps: When discussing challenges or performance issues, collaborate with your team members to identify potential solutions and develop action steps for improvement. Empower them to take ownership of their growth and development by encouraging their input and involvement in the decision-making process.

7\. Follow Up on Commitments and Progress: During subsequent one-on-one meetings, review the action steps and commitments from previous discussions to ensure that progress is being made. This accountability helps to reinforce the importance of the goals and expectations set during these meetings.

8\. Foster Career Development: Use one-on-one meetings to explore your team members' career aspirations and help them develop a roadmap for achieving their goals. This may involve discussing training opportunities, mentorship programs, or potential promotions within the organization.

9\. Adapt Your Approach: Recognize that each team member is unique, and tailor your approach to one-on-one meetings to suit their individual preferences, communication styles, and needs. This personalized approach can help to build rapport and create a more meaningful connection.

10\. Continuously Improve: Seek feedback from your team members on the effectiveness of your one-on-one meetings and be open to making adjustments to your approach as needed. This continuous improvement mindset demonstrates your commitment to effective leadership and supports a culture of growth and development.

#### Examples of questions to Ask During One-on-One Meetings

**General Check-In Questions**  
Begin every one-on-one meeting with a personal check-in. Understanding your team member’s mental state provides critical context for how to approach the rest of the conversation. These questions are essential to ask regularly, as they reveal insights into work-life balance, well-being, and personal circumstances. If an individual raises an issue, create space to explore it further. Flexibility is key here: adhering too rigidly to an agenda risks missing subtle cues that a team member needs support or guidance.

Examples of check-in questions:

* How are you feeling today? Walk me through your week.  
* What’s top of mind for you this week?  
* Last time we spoke, you mentioned X was challenging—how has that progressed?  
* What are your key priorities for the next seven days?

**Alignment Questions**  
Use this phase to gauge an individual’s confidence in and connection to the organization. These questions become especially vital after engagement surveys or major company shifts (e.g., leadership announcements, mergers, restructuring). Alignment discussions help identify disconnects early and reinforce shared purpose.

Examples:

* Do you have questions about the recent changes to X (e.g., the CEO’s announcement, team reorganization)?  
* How confident do you feel about the company’s current direction?  
* On a scale of 1–10, how aligned do you feel with our strategic priorities?

**Progress Questions**  
Understanding how team members perceive their growth—whether they feel momentum, fulfillment, or stagnation—is critical to sustaining engagement. Frame these discussions around both achievements and learning opportunities.

Examples:

* What energized you most in your role \[this week/month/quarter\]?  
* What challenges did you face \[recently\], and how did you navigate them?  
* What went exceptionally well or fell short of expectations?  
* What’s one new skill or insight you’ve gained recently?  
* How confident are you in your/your team’s progress toward goals?

**Relationship-Building Questions**  
Interpersonal dynamics often go unaddressed in one-on-ones, yet they profoundly impact job satisfaction and performance. Managers can use this time to coach team members on workplace relationships *and* model vulnerability by inviting feedback on their own leadership.

Examples:

* How would you describe your collaboration with \[team/colleague X\]?  
* Are there interactions you’d like to reflect on or improve?  
* What could I do differently to better support you?

**Career Aspiration Questions**  
Career conversations should occur far more frequently than annual reviews—ideally woven into regular check-ins. Proactive discussions signal investment in an individual’s growth and help align their trajectory with organizational opportunities.

Examples:

* When you envision yourself in two years, what does that look like?  
* What 2–3 skills would you like to develop in your current role?  
* How are you progressing toward your long-term career (or life) goals?  
* Is there someone in/outside the company you’d like to learn from?  
* What steps did you take this week toward your career objectives?

**Closing Questions**  
End each meeting with actionable clarity. These questions maintain accountability and keep feedback loops open:

* What key deliverables will you focus on before our next meeting?  
* How can I support you between now and our next conversation?  
* Is there anything we didn’t cover that you’d like to revisit later?

**A Note on Informal Feedback**  
While structured one-on-ones are invaluable, don’t reserve feedback for formal meetings. Offer timely, specific praise or guidance during casual interactions (e.g., after a presentation, in a Slack message). This continuous feedback culture reinforces psychological safety and ensures team members always know where they stand.

### Skip-levels Meetings {#skip-levels-meetings}

Skip-level meetings serve as a vital management tool, enabling senior leaders to engage with employees situated one or more levels beneath them in the organizational structure. These meetings promote open dialogue, uncover potential challenges, and provide valuable insights into the organization's overall health and performance. By grasping the significance and advantages of skip-level meetings and applying effective strategies, leaders can cultivate an atmosphere that supports transparency, growth, and development.

#### Purpose and Benefits of Skip-Level Meetings

* **Gain a Broader Perspective:** Skip-level meetings allow leaders to understand the experiences, challenges, and viewpoints of employees at various levels within the organization. This wider perspective can assist leaders in making better-informed decisions and fostering a more inclusive and supportive workplace culture.  
    
* **Identify and Address Issues:** These meetings create a chance to reveal any potential concerns or problems that might not have reached higher management through standard communication channels. Leaders can then take proactive steps to tackle these issues and implement necessary changes.  
    
* **Enhance Communication:** Skip-level meetings encourage open communication and dialogue, helping to dismantle hierarchical barriers and promote a more collaborative work environment. Employees may feel more at ease sharing their thoughts and ideas with senior leaders, knowing their voices are valued.

* **Boost Employee Engagement:** By engaging directly with employees, leaders can demonstrate their commitment to employee well-being, growth, and development. This can help to enhance employee engagement and satisfaction, leading to higher levels of motivation, productivity, and retention.  
    
* **Strengthen Leadership Skills:** Skip-level meetings provide an opportunity for leaders to hone their listening, communication, and problem-solving skills, as well as to develop a deeper understanding of the organization and its various functions.

#### Best Practices for Effective Skip-Level Meetings

By adopting these best practices, leaders can facilitate effective and impactful skip-level meetings that drive overall success.

* Schedule Regularly: Make it a point to have skip-level meetings on a regular basis, like every three or six months. This regularity fosters trust and strengthens relationships between leaders and their teams.  
    
* Set Clear Objectives: Make sure to clearly outline the goals and purpose of the skip-level meeting for everyone involved. This way, all participants can align their expectations and come prepared.  
    
* Encourage Open Dialogue: Foster a culture that supports open and honest discussions, encouraging employees to voice their thoughts, ideas, and concerns freely, without worrying about negative consequences.

* Listen Actively: Make it a priority to genuinely listen to your employees and grasp their viewpoints, instead of steering the conversation or becoming overly defensive. Use active listening techniques like summarizing, reflecting, and posing open-ended questions.  
    
* Address Concerns: Take steps to resolve any issues or concerns that come up during the skip-level meeting. This shows your dedication to tackling problems and supporting the well-being of your team members.  
    
* Follow Up: After the meeting, reach out to employees to confirm that any action items have been taken care of and to gather feedback on how effective the skip-level meeting was.  
    
* Maintain Confidentiality: It's important to respect employees' privacy by keeping sensitive information shared during meetings confidential and ensuring it is handled with care.  
    
* Provide Feedback: Give employees constructive feedback on their performance and offer guidance on how they can further their growth and development within the organization.  
    
* Be Approachable: Create an environment of approachability and openness by being genuine, empathetic, and non-judgmental in your interactions with employees.  
    
* Evaluate and Adapt: Regularly assess how effective your skip-level meetings are and be willing to make changes as necessary to ensure they continue to achieve their goals and support the growth of your team members.

## Team Bonding {#team-bonding}

Fostering connection in remote teams goes beyond formal meetings and work tasks—it requires creative approaches to replicate the casual interactions of an office environment. ### The Power of Donut Chats in remote teams {#the-power-of-donut-chats-in-remote-teams}

In a remote work environment, the chances for spontaneous, water-cooler conversations are limited. These casual interactions often help build meaningful relationships and enhance team cohesion, which is essential for effective performance. However, without a physical office, creating such connections can be difficult. This is where Donut chats come into play, providing a structured yet relaxed way to encourage camaraderie and communication among remote teams.

Donut chats, named after the well-known Slack plugin 'Donut' (https://www.donut.com/), randomly pair team members for one-on-one, informal discussions. The aim is to cultivate organic connections and initiate conversations that go beyond work-related tasks and projects.

These chats offer a chance to learn about each other's lives outside of work, share hobbies, interests, and experiences, or simply enjoy a laugh over a virtual coffee. They help break down hierarchical barriers, allowing everyone from interns to CEOs to participate, which fosters a culture of openness and inclusivity.

This practice promotes empathy by providing insights into the challenges and successes that colleagues encounter in both their personal and professional lives. It also facilitates the exchange of ideas and viewpoints that might not surface in formal meetings, encouraging creativity and innovation.

Companies like Zapier, which operates entirely remotely, have successfully adopted Donut chats. Each week, their team members are randomly paired for a Donut chat, regardless of their roles or departments. According to Zapier’s CEO Wade Foster, these conversations have nurtured a culture of empathy and understanding, significantly contributing to the company’s overall productivity and success.

Another company that makes use of Donut chats is the software firm Buffer. They employ the Donut tool to randomly pair team members for video chats. These virtual coffee breaks have become a vital part of Buffer's culture, fostering meaningful connections among its distributed team. However, it's crucial to keep in mind that while Donut chats offer many advantages, participation should be optional. Not every employee may feel at ease with one-on-one conversations, and that's perfectly fine. The aim is to create an opportunity, not to impose a requirement.

In summary, Donut chats present an innovative approach to addressing one of the challenges of remote work – the absence of casual, spontaneous interactions. By encouraging informal communication, nurturing empathy, and dismantling hierarchical barriers, Donut chats can play a significant role in building a strong, cohesive remote team. As demonstrated by the experiences of Zapier and Buffer, when implemented with consideration for individual preferences, Donut chats can reshape the dynamics of remote teams, enhancing productivity and fostering a more inclusive work culture.

# Giving and Receiving Feedback {#giving-and-receiving-feedback}

Feedback, which involves sharing observations, insights, and suggestions between two parties, is a crucial element in professional development and growth. It is a key part of the organizational ecosystem, supporting the communication channels that promote continuous improvement and nurturing a culture of shared learning and understanding. When used effectively, feedback can be a powerful tool for managers, helping to develop individuals and teams, improve performance, and create an environment of open communication and mutual respect.

One of the main benefits of feedback is its ability to enhance performance at all levels within an organization. It provides a way for employees to reflect on and improve their skills, behaviors, and attitudes. When feedback is exchanged frequently, it offers valuable insights into leadership styles and employee performance, ultimately leading to better decision-making. It also empowers employees by giving them a platform to express their views and concerns, which contributes to a more engaged and productive workforce.

Moreover, feedback is essential for personal and professional development. Rather than following a "one-size-fits-all" approach, feedback allows for a more tailored strategy that considers each individual's unique strengths and areas for growth. This encourages employees to take ownership of their development, reducing the constant need for human resources to initiate the process. Encouraging employees to seek feedback, especially after important events or projects, can provide timely and relevant insights that are vital for their professional journey.

Additionally, a strong feedback culture can help prevent potential conflicts from escalating. It equips individuals with the skills to express their concerns or issues in a constructive and respectful way. As a result, individuals become more comfortable having difficult conversations, reducing the likelihood of misunderstandings and fostering a harmonious workplace environment.  
Feedback is also strongly correlated with increased employee engagement. It not only offers a mechanism to quickly resolve issues and foster knowledge sharing but also provides an avenue to regularly acknowledge and celebrate team and individual achievements. When employees feel recognized and appreciated for their efforts, they are more likely to feel engaged and motivated, resulting in increased productivity and job satisfaction 1 .  
Despite its numerous benefits, the act of giving feedback can be challenging. It requires a thoughtful approach to ensure the feedback is both effective and well-received. Various strategies and techniques can be employed to maximize the effectiveness of feedback.

**Establish Regular Feedback Sessions**  
You should establish regular check-ins with your direct reports, in addition to the formal performance review process. These could be weekly or bi-weekly meetings, where you discuss current projects, challenges, and progress. This is an opportunity to give and receive ongoing feedback, keeping everyone on the same page and ensuring small issues don't escalate.

**The 'SBI' Method: Situation, Behavior, Impact**  
When giving feedback, a useful approach is the SBI method. You describe the situation where the behavior occurred, the behavior itself, and the impact it had. This is a clear and direct way to communicate feedback, focusing on specific events and behaviors, and avoids personal interpretations or judgments.

For example: "In yesterday's team meeting (situation), I noticed you interrupted colleagues a few times while they were speaking (behavior). This can make others feel unheard and hinder our collaborative efforts (impact).” https://hbr.org/2019/05/what-good-feedback-really-looks-like

**Describe the work rather than the person**  
This is essential for maintaining objectivity and focusing on performance rather than personal traits. It also helps prevent potential bias and ensures the feedback is about professional development.

**Balance Positive and Constructive Feedback**  
While it's important to provide feedback on areas for improvement, don't overlook the importance of positive feedback. Recognizing and appreciating your direct report's good work builds their confidence and reinforces the behaviors and outcomes you want to see. https://www.betterup.com/blog/benefits-of-feedback  
This is important for avoiding "feedback inflation". Recognizing exceptional work can motivate employees, but overpraising expected tasks can lower standards and impact motivation.

**Be Specific and Actionable**  
When giving feedback, be as specific as possible. Vague feedback can be confusing and isn't helpful for improvement. Also, make sure your feedback is actionable \- it should lead to a clear course of action your direct report can take to improve. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30102068/

**Encourage Self-Reflection**  
Before providing your feedback, you could ask your direct report for their perspective. How do they think they performed? What do they think they did well, and where could they improve? This encourages self-reflection and opens up a dialogue, rather than a one-way communication.

**The 'Feedforward' Technique**  
Marshall Goldsmith's 'feedforward' technique involves giving suggestions for the future rather than feedback on past actions. It's a positive and future-focused method that helps people envision and work towards their improved future self.

For example: "In future meetings, I'd suggest that you make a conscious effort to allow others to finish their thoughts before chiming in. This will facilitate better discussion flow and teamwork."

**Remember the Feedback Sandwich**  
The 'feedback sandwich' is a method where you sandwich a piece of constructive feedback between two pieces of positive feedback. This helps ensure the conversation remains balanced and doesn't feel overwhelmingly negative. However, use this method carefully as some people may see it as insincere or evasive.

### 

**Follow-Up**  
After giving feedback, it's important to follow up to reinforce that the goal is to support the team member's development and success. This also gives you an opportunity to see how they're implementing the feedback and whether they need any further support.

**Review the review process periodically**   
It's important to continuously improve the review process itself based on feedback from all stakeholders, including HR and the employees being reviewed. This ensures the process remains relevant, efficient, and effective.

In addition to these strategies, finally, remember that providing feedback is a skill that needs to be developed. As you continue to practice giving and receiving feedback, you'll become more effective in your communication and in supporting your team's growth and development.

## Handling difficult situations \- Real life Example {#handling-difficult-situations---real-life-example}

### Scenario One {#scenario-one}

During a routine one-on-one session with one of your direct reports, they express frustration over an interaction with a teammate. When you request more details, your report provides a link to a recent code review. In this review, they had posted a critique on a minor detail—something small and arguably inconsequential—aimed at refining the code’s quality. Although their comment wasn’t meant to be personal, the tone came across as overly pedantic, and this appears to have triggered a negative reaction from the pull request’s author.

In response, the PR author—who is also a member of your team—fired back a remark that was dismissive and personal, implying that while your report might be skilled as an engineer, they are ill-suited to conducting code reviews. This escalation has led your report to feel both misunderstood and unfairly attacked.

You now find yourself in a position where both individuals work under your guidance, and the dynamic has become strained.

**How would you approach this situation?**

The goal is to de-escalate tension and promote constructive communication.

During the one-on-one, I would begin by acknowledging the engineer’s frustration, showing empathy and understanding. A simple statement like, “I can see why this situation would be frustrating,” helps them feel heard and reassured that their concerns are taken seriously.

Next, I would mention that while their comments in the code review weren’t meant to be personal, nitpicking can sometimes come off as overly critical and lead to misunderstandings. I’d encourage them to keep the big picture in mind during code reviews, emphasizing the importance of constructive feedback that adds value to the project without getting bogged down in minor details. I might even suggest specific language or approaches for future reviews, such as, “In a separate PR, we could enhance this part by making these changes or by following this approach.”

Then, I’d schedule a separate one-on-one with the PR author as soon as possible, making it clear that personal comments in professional settings aren’t helpful. I’d encourage them to take feedback in stride, understanding that code review comments are meant to improve the code, not to critique them personally. I’d also suggest ways to seek clarification when feedback seems confusing or overly specific, rather than reacting defensively.

In the next team meeting, I would remind everyone of our code review principles: focus on constructive feedback, avoid nitpicking, and communicate respectfully. Without pointing fingers, this serves as a broader message to reinforce expectations around feedback and create a shared understanding of review etiquette.

If necessary, I’d encourage both engineers to have an open, respectful conversation. I could suggest that they clarify feedback expectations moving forward to foster mutual understanding and a stronger collaborative dynamic. I’d offer to facilitate this conversation if needed, aiming to rebuild trust and promote a more supportive approach to future code reviews.

### Scenario Two {#scenario-two}

At Apex Solutions, engineering managers don’t directly drive daily development efforts; instead, they depend on regular updates from project managers, who work closely with engineers and remain deeply engaged in the day-to-day progress. These ongoing check-ins between the EM and PM are designed to facilitate the flow of information, ensuring that both parties have the insights needed to support their shared teams effectively. On occasion, as an additional touchpoint, an EM might participate silently in a team meeting or stand-up just to understand the engineers’ experiences first-hand.

The scenario  
In a recent sync-up, your project manager contact mentions that one of your direct reports has not been performing at their usual standard for over a week. Previously known for active participation in team discussions and steady task completion, this engineer now seems quieter and less engaged. Moreover, their pace of completing assigned tasks has noticeably decreased, raising concerns that something might be amiss.

This feedback catches you off guard. Just a couple of days ago, you spoke with the engineer in question and perceived nothing out of the ordinary. They provided no hint of trouble, seemed motivated, and appeared to be progressing as they always had. Now, you’re tasked with reconciling the project manager’s observations of a performance dip with your recent impression that everything was running smoothly.

**How would you approach this situation?**

In this situation, my first step would be to acknowledge the Project Manager’s feedback and thank them for bringing it to my attention. I would ask for specific examples of what they’ve observed, such as instances where the engineer didn’t participate actively or particular tasks where their velocity dropped. This would help me gain a clearer perspective on the issue and understand any patterns. Since I recently spoke with the engineer and didn’t notice anything unusual, I’d approach the situation with empathy and an open mind. I’d set up a quick check-in with them, framing it as a routine follow-up to discuss how they’re feeling about their current workload and any challenges they might be facing. I’d aim to keep the conversation casual and supportive to ensure they feel comfortable sharing if something is affecting their performance. During the check-in, I’d ask open-ended questions like, “How are things going on your end with the current project?” or “Is there anything in your workload that’s felt challenging lately?” By creating a safe space, the engineer may be more inclined to share if they’re facing any difficulties, whether work-related or personal.

If the engineer opens up about a particular challenge or reason for their change in engagement, I’d work with them to address it, whether that involves adjusting their workload, providing additional support, or collaborating with the Project Manager to make accommodations. On the other hand, if they don’t identify any specific issues, I’d reassure them that I’m here for support and encourage them to reach out if anything comes up.

Finally, I’d maintain close communication with the Project Manager, updating them that I’ve followed up with the engineer while respecting confidentiality. I’d also keep an eye on the situation in the coming weeks, perhaps by joining a team ceremony as a listener to observe firsthand. This would allow me to gain further insights and provide the Project Manager with any relevant feedback to ensure we’re both aligned.

### Scenario Three {#scenario-three}

At Acme, the engineering career path clearly delineates five individual contributor levels, each representing a different scope of responsibility and impact:

* **WordPress Engineer**

Those serving as Tech Leads guide technical decisions and ensure the engineering team operates cohesively and efficiently during that project’s lifecycle.

The scenario  
Now, having joined Acme just a month ago, you’ve made it a priority to understand the engineers on your team. With these insights in hand, you’re ready to embark on your first round of career development conversations.

Your next conversation is scheduled with a highly capable and intelligent engineer who currently holds the position of “Senior WordPress Engineer.” Impressively, this individual has already stepped into the Tech Lead role several times, successfully guiding project teams to effective technical solutions and demonstrating leadership potential beyond their official title. **How would you prepare for this “career development conversation”?**

To prepare for the career development conversation with the Senior WordPress Engineer, I would take a structured and personalized approach to ensure the discussion is both meaningful and supportive of their growth. Here’s how I would approach it:

First, I would review Acme criteria for advancing within the engineering titles, particularly focusing on the transition from “Senior Engineer” to “Staff Engineer.” Understanding the specific competencies, behaviors, and impact required for progression would help me provide clear and actionable advice.

Next, I would gather performance data and past feedback from their Project Managers, along with insights from recent projects where they’ve acted as Tech Lead. This would allow me to assess their strengths, leadership capabilities, and areas where they’ve excelled, giving context to our conversation.

To foster an open and exploratory discussion, I would prepare questions that focus on their career goals, especially if they’re interested in broader roles involving mentorship or contributing to company-wide initiatives. If they express interest in advancing toward a Staff Engineer role, I would be ready to discuss resources like advanced technical training or leadership coaching that could aid their development.

“What excites you the most about your current role, and where would you like to grow further?”

“How would you feel about taking on responsibilities beyond project delivery, such as mentorship or contributing to company-wide initiatives?”

“Are there specific areas where you’d appreciate additional support or resources to help reach your next career milestone?”

### Scenario Four {#scenario-four}

At Acme, the “Tech Lead” designation isn’t tied to rank or seniority; it’s a role assigned on a project-by-project basis, granting an engineer responsibility for guiding technical choices. Finally, I’d offer to check in regularly to see how things are going and provide any additional support they might need along the way. Knowing they have a bit of backup can go a long way in building confidence and making this new role feel more manageable.

# The Dark Side of Smart Working {#the-dark-side-of-smart-working}

## Lack of social interaction {#lack-of-social-interaction}

One of the biggest challenges of smart working is the lack of social interaction. Employees who work from home often miss out on the social aspects of being in an office, such as chatting with colleagues, sharing ideas, and collaborating on projects. This can lead to feelings of loneliness and isolation, which can have a negative impact on mental health and job satisfaction.

Isolation and loneliness can have a significant impact on remote workers, affecting various aspects of their work life and well-being. A reduced sense of community can arise when remote workers feel disconnected from their organization and colleagues, resulting in feelings of isolation and being "out of the loop." This disconnection can lead to increased stress and anxiety, with some individuals feeling pressured to prove their worth or overworking themselves in order to feel connected or productive.

Additionally, isolation and loneliness can impair work performance, causing difficulty concentrating, lower motivation levels, and a lack of energy that can impact the ability to complete tasks and meet deadlines. Remote workers may also lack the social support that in-person contact with colleagues provides, leading to decreased job satisfaction and engagement. Finally, isolation can have physical health consequences, such as poor sleep, a lack of exercise, and unhealthy eating habits, all of which can negatively affect overall health and well-being.

### Physical health impacts {#physical-health-impacts}

The physical health impacts of remote work on employees have become increasingly important to address as more people shift to this work arrangement. In our experience and study, we identified several common health issues affecting remote workers:

* Poor sleep: Remote workers may struggle with insomnia or have difficulty falling asleep or staying asleep, which can impact their productivity and focus during the day. According to the National Sleep Foundation, poor sleep can also negatively affect mood, memory, and overall health.  
* Lack of exercise: Without a regular commute or office routine, remote workers may find it challenging to maintain consistent exercise habits. This can lead to physical health impacts such as weight gain, decreased cardiovascular health, and reduced overall fitness. A sedentary lifestyle has been linked to numerous health problems, including obesity and heart disease (Mayo Clinic, 2020).  
* Unhealthy eating habits: Remote workers who are isolated may be more likely to engage in unhealthy eating habits, such as snacking on high-calorie foods or relying on convenience foods lacking nutritional value. These habits can increase the risk of obesity, heart disease, and diabetes (Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health, 2020).  
* Reduced immune function: Isolation and loneliness can have negative effects on the immune system, making remote workers more susceptible to illness and infection. Research has shown that social isolation can lead to decreased immune function and increased inflammation, contributing to various health problems (Cacioppo et al., 2015).  
* Increased stress: Isolation and loneliness can result in heightened stress levels, negatively impacting physical health. Chronic stress has been linked to a range of health problems, including heart disease, high blood pressure, and digestive disorders (American Psychological Association, 2020).

### Negative impacts on work performance {#negative-impacts-on-work-performance}

Isolation and loneliness can also have negative impacts on work performance for remote workers. Here are some of the potential impacts:

* Difficulty concentrating: Remote workers who are isolated may have difficulty focusing on their work, leading to decreased productivity and performance. Without the structure of a regular office environment, remote workers may be more susceptible to distractions, such as household chores or social media.  
* Lower motivation: Remote workers who feel isolated may experience a decrease in motivation, leading to decreased engagement and productivity. Without the social support and accountability provided by colleagues, remote workers may struggle to maintain their motivation over time.  
* Lack of energy: Isolation and loneliness can lead to decreased energy levels, which can impact work performance. Remote workers may feel fatigued or burned out, leading to decreased productivity and engagement.  
* Decreased collaboration: Remote workers who are isolated may struggle to collaborate effectively with colleagues, leading to decreased productivity and performance. Without regular in-person contact, remote workers may find it more difficult to communicate and collaborate effectively with others.  
* Increased procrastination: Isolation and loneliness can lead to increased procrastination, as remote workers may feel less accountable for their work and more susceptible to distractions. This can lead to decreased productivity and missed deadlines.

### Addressing the lack of social interaction {#addressing-the-lack-of-social-interaction}

Remote workers can address the lack of social interaction by adopting several proactive strategies:

* Schedule regular check-ins: By scheduling regular check-ins with colleagues and managers through phone, video calls, or instant messaging platforms, remote workers can maintain a sense of connection and accountability.  
* Join virtual communities: Participating in virtual communities or online forums related to their industry or interests provides remote workers with networking, idea-sharing, and social interaction opportunities.  
* Participate in virtual team-building activities: Engaging in virtual team-building activities, such as online games, virtual happy hours, or other interactive experiences, can help remote workers build camaraderie and foster a sense of team spirit.  
* Make time for in-person meetings: Whenever possible, remote workers should attend in-person meetings and events to maintain relationships and facilitate face-to-face interaction with colleagues.

While the lack of social interaction is a potential downside of remote working, proactive strategies can help remote workers maintain connections and build relationships despite working outside a traditional office environment.

Moreover, remote workers should create structure and routines in their workday to mitigate the physical health impacts of isolation. This can include setting specific goals and deadlines, breaking tasks into smaller, manageable chunks, and maintaining regular communication with colleagues. Prioritizing self-care, such as taking regular breaks, engaging in exercise, and focusing on a healthy diet and sleep, is also essential to maintaining energy and motivation levels.

By implementing these proactive steps, remote workers can combat the impacts of isolation and loneliness while sustaining their performance and productivity over time.

## Work-life Balance {#work-life-balance}

Another challenge of smart working is the difficulty of maintaining work-life balance. Without a clear separation between work and home life, it can be difficult to switch off from work and relax.

The absence of a clear separation between work and home life can make it challenging for employees to switch off from work and truly relax. When work tasks and responsibilities seep into personal time, employees may find it difficult to establish boundaries, leading to overworking and an inability to disconnect from their professional responsibilities.

This blurring of lines between work and personal life can contribute to burnout, stress, and decreased productivity in the long run. Burnout, a state of chronic emotional and mental exhaustion resulting from prolonged work-related stress, can have adverse effects on both the employees' well-being and the organization's overall performance.

Setting boundaries between work and personal life is crucial for maintaining a healthy work-life balance, especially in smart working arrangements. Establishing and respecting these boundaries can help prevent burnout, reduce stress, and improve overall well-being. Here are some practical examples and tips for setting boundaries:

**Designate a specific workspace:** Create a dedicated workspace in your home, separate from your living and relaxation areas. This physical separation can help you mentally separate work from personal life, making it easier to disconnect when you're not working.  
**Set a work schedule:** Establish a consistent work schedule with clear start and end times. Communicate this schedule with your colleagues and family members to ensure that they respect your work hours and personal time.  
**Take breaks and manage your energy:** Schedule regular breaks throughout the day, including a lunch break, to recharge and prevent overworking. Taking short walks or engaging in relaxation exercises can help you maintain your energy and focus.  
**Turn off notifications during non-work hours:** Disable work-related notifications on your devices during non-work hours to avoid being constantly reminded of work tasks and responsibilities. By doing so, you can be more present in your personal life and reduce the temptation to check work emails and messages during your downtime.  
**Establish a wind-down routine:** Create a routine to signal the end of the workday, such as closing your laptop, tidying your workspace, or engaging in a relaxation activity. This routine can help you transition from work mode to personal mode, making it easier to disconnect from work.  
**Prioritize self-care:** Make time for self-care activities, such as exercise, hobbies, and spending time with friends and family. These activities can help you recharge and maintain a sense of well-being outside of work.  
**Communicate your boundaries:** Be open and clear with your colleagues, managers, and family members about your boundaries and expectations regarding work and personal time. Clear communication can help ensure that everyone understands and respects your boundaries.
